## Key Observations

### 1. Framing: Technology Policy, Not Climate Science
Nuclear and renewables coverage is framed almost exclusively as an energy-policy and technology-investment question, not as a climate science question. The dominant speaker type is M6 (politicians), who together account for 221 of the ~295 classified segments. Scientists (M3) appear in only 5 segments — the lowest scientist presence of all three case studies relative to corpus size. This means the core question ("which low-carbon technologies should Czech energy policy favour?") is answered by political actors, not scientific ones.

### 2. Delayer Framing Is Characteristic Here
CS1 has the highest Delayer (S3) rate of the three case studies: 22 M6-Delayer segments. The dominant delayer argument across the excerpts is temporal — nuclear as an unavoidable "bridge" before renewables can scale, and the impossibility of decarbonising by 2030 without existing nuclear. Minister Havlíček's statement (Excerpt 5: "nejsme schopni přestrojit energetiku do roku 2030 když jádro nám tady bude vznikat až ve třicátých letech") exemplifies this frame: urgency is acknowledged but used to justify slower action.

### 3. EU Taxonomy as Turning Point
A cluster of 2022 documents (Excerpts 1–3) reflects the EU taxonomy debate over classifying nuclear and gas as "sustainable transition" energy sources. ČT coverage gave prominent voice to Czech and French pro-nuclear positions alongside Austrian and German opposition. Věra Jourová's quote — "jaderná energie sice není zelená energie ale pro dlouhou dobu ji uznáme jako vhodnou pro investice" — exemplifies how Czech public broadcasting framed the taxonomy outcome as a victory, reflecting the Czech government's position.

### 4. Renewables Treated as Competitor, Not Complement
Wind and solar appear mainly as foils to nuclear in the coverage, not as independently developing technologies. The framing rarely presents a mixed-energy scenario; instead, nuclear vs. renewables is structured as a binary political choice. This mirrors the Czech policy debate but may misrepresent the technical reality of energy transition.

### 5. Temporal Pattern: Event-Driven, Not Sustained
Coverage is concentrated in 2019 (26 docs) and 2021–2022 (33 docs combined), driven by the Global Climate Strike / Green New Deal moment and then the EU taxonomy and Ukraine energy crises. Between 2012–2018, coverage averages under 3 docs per year. The beat does not reflect sustained editorial engagement with energy transition; it reflects reactive coverage of external policy events.
