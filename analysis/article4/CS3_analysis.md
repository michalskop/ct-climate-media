## Key Observations

### 1. The Silence Is the Finding
Only 17 of 2,914 climate documents (0.6%) explicitly link climate change to human health impacts. This is not a marginal omission — the scientific literature on climate-health links is extensive (heat mortality, respiratory illness from air pollution, vector-borne disease range expansion, malnutrition from crop failures). ČT's climate coverage simply does not make this connection. Health consequences of climate change are not a recognised journalistic beat.

### 2. What Little Exists Is Event-Driven, Not Thematic
The 17 documents do not form a thematic thread. They are scattered across nine years with no year exceeding 4 documents and no discernible trend toward more coverage over time. Each document appears to be an isolated response to a specific event (an IPCC report, a heat wave, an expert interview segment) rather than part of sustained health-climate journalism.

### 3. The Best Examples Come from Science Interviews
The most explicit climate-health nexus language appears in structured expert interviews — Excerpt 5 (2017: Bedřich Moldan on health consequences of climate impacts) and Excerpt 3 (2014: IPCC report framing — "teplejší, sušší a hladovější"). These are format-driven inclusions: scientists, given space to elaborate, make the climate-health link. The link does not appear in news report framing itself.

### 4. Contrast with CS2 Reveals the Gap
CS2 covers heat waves extensively (586 documents). CS3 shows that heat waves almost never appear alongside health impact framing. The same vlna veder events that generated hundreds of drought/agriculture/weather documents generated almost no climate-health linkage. Heat is covered as a meteorological and agricultural problem, not as a public health emergency.

### 5. Speaker Profile Reflects the Silence
The M6-Denier presence (2 segments) in a 17-document corpus is disproportionately high relative to CS1 and CS2. This may reflect that on the rare occasions when climate-health links are raised, political resistance surfaces. The tiny M2 (citizen) presence — only 1 segment — confirms that affected individuals are not interviewed about health consequences; this remains an elite-and-expert discourse even within the small set of relevant documents.

### 6. Methodological Note on Scope
The 17-document count uses strict regex co-occurrence requiring climate AND health terms to appear in proximity within the same document. This deliberately excludes documents that mention both topics independently (e.g., a document covering heat wave weather AND separately mentioning a health ministry budget). The strict filter is appropriate for measuring genuine climate-health linkage in editorial framing, not accidental co-occurrence.
