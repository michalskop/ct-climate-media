## Key Observations

### 1. The Largest and Most Diverse Case Study
With 586 documents (20.1% of the climate corpus), drought and floods is the dominant empirical topic in ČT climate coverage. The speaker profile is correspondingly more diverse: M2 citizens appear in 172 segments (neutral + informer), the highest citizen presence of the three case studies. This reflects the lived, local nature of extreme weather — affected farmers, residents, and eyewitnesses are regularly interviewed alongside officials.

### 2. Climate Attribution: Present but Backgrounded
The documents consistently cover extreme weather events, but explicit climate attribution ("caused by climate change") is often reserved for expert M3 segments rather than the journalistic framing of news items. Excerpt 2 (2016) illustrates this: the lead is drought-as-local-water-crisis ("Česko pomalu ale jistě vysychá"), with climate context left to be drawn by the viewer. Excerpt 3 is notable as a counterexample — a scientist explicitly links heat wave mortality to climate-driven patterns, framing climate as a public-safety issue.

### 3. Adaptation Framing Dominates Over Mitigation
Topics T10 (Drought water agriculture), T12 (Forest crisis / bark beetle), and T18 (Floods & extreme weather) all appear here. The adaptation frame — how to manage the consequences of climate change — is clearly dominant. Mitigation (reducing emissions to prevent future events) is largely absent. This is consistent with Finding 7 from Phase 3: adaptation-topic documents peaked in 2018–2019 (the Czech drought emergency years), treating climate as something to cope with rather than to prevent.

### 4. Peak in 2019 Reflects Czech Domestic Crisis, Not Global Activism
Unlike CS1 where the 2019 peak reflects international climate movement coverage, CS2's 2019 peak (130 docs) is driven primarily by the second consecutive severe Czech drought year, bark beetle forest crisis, and Central European heat waves. This is domestically embedded climate reporting — grounded in real agricultural and ecological emergency rather than following a global event calendar.

### 5. Delayer and Denier Voices Are Minimal Here
Only 6 M6-Denier and 18 M6-Delayer segments appear in CS2, which is proportionally low relative to the 586-document corpus. Extreme weather coverage produces fewer politically contested frames — the weather itself is undeniable. This contrasts with CS1 (energy policy) where delay framing is prominent. The practical-consequence framing of drought and flood coverage effectively forecloses denial.

### 6. The Missing Link: Justice and Vulnerability
Despite the large coverage volume, climate justice framing is absent. Affected populations are local Czech farmers, foresters, and flood victims — the most climate-vulnerable global communities (small island states, Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia) do not appear in this case study. The suffering is proximate; the structural question of who caused climate change and who suffers most is never raised.
